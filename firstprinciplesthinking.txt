ID: FP-NODE-001
TAGS: First Principles, Core Definition, Fundamental Thinking
DATE: 2026-03-22
CONFIDENCE: High
--- SYNOPSIS ---
First principles thinking is the practice of breaking down complex problems
into their most basic, irreducible truths and reasoning upward from there
to build new understanding or solutions.
--- CORE ANALYSIS ---
# ROOT CAUSE: Why People Don't Think This Way Naturally
The human brain defaults to reasoning by analogy — copying what worked before.
First principles is effortful because it demands that every assumption be
justified from scratch rather than inherited.
# PATTERN: The Reconstruction Loop
Strip assumptions → identify bedrock facts → rebuild solution upward.
Every step in the rebuild must be justified by a fundamental truth, not
by convention or precedent.
# ORIGIN
Aristotle defined a first principle as "the first basis from which a thing
is known." Elon Musk popularized its modern application: boiling things down
to fundamental truths and reasoning up from there rather than by analogy.
--- DATA POINTS ---
- Key Question: What do we know for certain is true?
- Key Question: What assumptions are we making that may not be necessary?
- Key Question: If we stripped everything away, what would remain?
- Characteristic: Challenges inherited knowledge
- Characteristic: Separates facts from beliefs
- Characteristic: Constructs ideas from ground up
- Characteristic: Slow and effortful but produces genuinely novel insights

ID: FP-NODE-002
TAGS: Root Cause Analysis, RCA, Problem Solving, Diagnosis
DATE: 2026-03-22
CONFIDENCE: High
--- SYNOPSIS ---
Root cause analysis is a problem-solving method that identifies the
underlying cause of a problem rather than treating its symptoms. It asks
not just what happened but why it happened and why that happened.
--- CORE ANALYSIS ---
# ROOT CAUSE: Surface vs Deep Diagnosis
Most problem solving stops at the symptom level because symptoms are visible
and causes are hidden. RCA refuses to accept any explanation that still has
a "why" unanswered beneath it.
# PATTERN: The 5 Whys Chain
Ask why five times in succession. Each answer becomes the subject of the
next why. The fifth answer typically reveals a systemic cause rather than
a surface event.
# DIFFERENCE FROM FIRST PRINCIPLES
RCA is diagnostic — it works backward from a known problem.
First principles thinking is generative — it works forward from known facts
to build new solutions. Both reject surface explanations and demand depth.
--- DATA POINTS ---
- Techniques: 5 Whys, Fishbone (Ishikawa) Diagram, Fault Tree Analysis
- Direction: Backward from known problem
- Goal: Prevent recurrence by fixing the actual cause
- Related: First principles thinking, systems thinking

ID: FP-NODE-003
TAGS: Pattern Recognition, Analysis, Scientific Discovery
DATE: 2026-03-22
CONFIDENCE: High
--- SYNOPSIS ---
Pattern recognition is the cognitive ability to identify regularities,
repetitions, and structures across data, events, or observations. It is
the foundation of scientific discovery, strategic thinking, and creative insight.
--- CORE ANALYSIS ---
# ROOT CAUSE: Why Patterns Matter
Reality repeats itself at different scales and across different domains.
The same underlying principle manifests in physics, economics, biology,
and human behavior. Pattern recognition reveals these hidden connections.
# PATTERN: Cross-Domain Transfer
Once a pattern is identified in one domain it can be applied to another.
Evolution in biology becomes iteration in software. Supply and demand in
economics becomes attention in social media.
# TYPES OF PATTERNS
Structural: recurring shapes or relationships such as networks and hierarchies.
Temporal: events that repeat over time such as cycles, trends, and seasonality.
Causal: if X then Y cause and effect chains.
Analogical: different domains sharing the same underlying logic.
Anomaly: what breaks the pattern is often more revealing than the pattern itself.
--- DATA POINTS ---
- Application: Scientific discovery
- Application: Strategic planning
- Application: Creative problem solving
- Key Insight: Anomalies reveal more than patterns themselves

ID: FP-NODE-004
TAGS: Mental Models, Cognitive Frameworks, Decision Making
DATE: 2026-03-22
CONFIDENCE: High
--- SYNOPSIS ---
Mental models are simplified representations of how something works. They
are the cognitive frameworks through which people interpret the world and
make decisions under uncertainty.
--- CORE ANALYSIS ---
# ROOT CAUSE: Why Mental Models Matter
No one can hold the full complexity of reality in their mind. Mental models
are compression tools — they sacrifice detail for usability. The quality of
your decisions depends on the quality of your mental models.
# PATTERN: The Latticework
Charlie Munger advocates building a latticework of mental models from
multiple disciplines. A problem viewed through only one model produces
narrow solutions. Viewed through many models it reveals deeper structure.
# KEY MENTAL MODELS
Supply and demand explains resource allocation across all domains.
Natural selection explains how systems improve through variation and pressure.
Compounding explains exponential growth in finance, knowledge, and habits.
Second-order effects explain why obvious solutions often backfire.
The map is not the territory explains why models always distort reality.
--- DATA POINTS ---
- Origin: Charlie Munger latticework concept
- Key Principle: More models = better decisions
- Risk: Over-relying on one model produces blind spots

ID: FP-NODE-005
TAGS: Inversion Thinking, Problem Solving, Charlie Munger
DATE: 2026-03-22
CONFIDENCE: High
--- SYNOPSIS ---
Inversion thinking approaches a problem by considering its opposite.
Instead of asking how to succeed, ask what would cause failure. Instead
of what makes a good product, ask what makes a product people hate.
--- CORE ANALYSIS ---
# ROOT CAUSE: Why Inversion Works
The brain is better at identifying threats and failures than at generating
positive solutions. Inversion exploits this by deliberately thinking about
the negative, then reversing the findings into positive insight.
# PATTERN: Failure Pre-Mortem
Before starting a project imagine it has already failed catastrophically.
List every reason it could have failed. Then build defenses against each
reason before the project even starts.
# CONNECTION TO FIRST PRINCIPLES
Inversion is a direction of travel within first principles reasoning.
It forces examination of assumptions from the opposite direction,
revealing blind spots that forward reasoning consistently misses.
--- DATA POINTS ---
- Popularized by: Charlie Munger
- Key Question: What would cause this to fail?
- Key Question: What makes the worst version of this?
- Application: Risk analysis, product design, strategy

ID: FP-NODE-006
TAGS: Systems Thinking, Emergence, Complex Systems
DATE: 2026-03-22
CONFIDENCE: High
--- SYNOPSIS ---
Systems thinking is the discipline of understanding how components of a
system interact, influence each other, and produce emergent behavior that
cannot be understood by looking at parts in isolation.
--- CORE ANALYSIS ---
# ROOT CAUSE: Why Reductionism Alone Fails
Breaking a system into parts reveals the parts but destroys the relationships.
A car engine understood part by part does not explain traffic. Systems
thinking restores the relationships that reductionism removes.
# PATTERN: Emergence
The whole behaves differently from the sum of its parts. Traffic jams
emerge from individual driving decisions. Market crashes emerge from
individual investment decisions. Pandemics emerge from individual contact.
# COMPLEMENT TO FIRST PRINCIPLES
First principles breaks things apart to find fundamentals.
Systems thinking reassembles the relationships between fundamentals.
A complete analytical framework uses both together.
--- DATA POINTS ---
- Key Concept: Emergence — whole behaves differently from sum of parts
- Key Concept: Feedback loops — outputs become inputs
- Key Concept: Nonlinearity — small changes produce large effects
- Complement: Reductionism finds parts, systems thinking finds relationships

ID: FP-NODE-007
TAGS: Brainstorming, SCAMPER, Idea Generation, Creativity
DATE: 2026-03-22
CONFIDENCE: High
--- SYNOPSIS ---
SCAMPER is a structured brainstorming checklist that forces creative
perspectives on any subject by systematically applying seven thinking
lenses to a problem or existing solution.
--- CORE ANALYSIS ---
# ROOT CAUSE: Why Structure Helps Creativity
Unstructured brainstorming defaults to familiar ideas. SCAMPER forces
the brain into unfamiliar angles by making each lens mandatory.
# PATTERN: Forced Perspective Rotation
Each SCAMPER lens is a forced question that cannot be skipped.
This prevents the group from stopping at comfortable ideas.
# THE SEVEN LENSES
Substitute: What components can be replaced with something else?
Combine: What elements can be merged to create something new?
Adapt: What can be modified or borrowed from another context?
Modify/Magnify/Minimize: What changes if scale or form changes?
Put to other uses: What other applications does this have?
Eliminate: What happens if a component is removed entirely?
Reverse/Rearrange: What if the sequence or orientation is flipped?
--- DATA POINTS ---
- Type: Structured brainstorming checklist
- Best for: Improving existing solutions
- Strength: Forces non-obvious perspectives
- Weakness: Less effective for completely new problem spaces

ID: FP-NODE-008
TAGS: Six Thinking Hats, De Bono, Parallel Thinking, Brainstorming
DATE: 2026-03-22
CONFIDENCE: High
--- SYNOPSIS ---
Edward de Bono's Six Thinking Hats assigns six distinct thinking modes
to six metaphorical hats, forcing a group to examine a problem from
multiple angles rather than letting one mode dominate.
--- CORE ANALYSIS ---
# ROOT CAUSE: Why Group Thinking Gets Stuck
In groups, one mode of thinking — usually critical or emotional — dominates
and crowds out others. Six Hats enforces parallel thinking where everyone
applies the same lens simultaneously.
# PATTERN: Sequential Lens Application
The group puts on each hat in sequence. While wearing a hat everyone
thinks only in that mode. This separates ego from thinking style and
prevents defensive argumentation.
# THE SIX HATS
White: Pure facts and data only. What do we know?
Red: Emotions and intuitions. How does this feel?
Black: Critical judgment and caution. What could go wrong?
Yellow: Optimism and best case. What is the upside?
Green: Creativity and new ideas. What alternatives exist?
Blue: Process control and meta-thinking. How are we thinking?
--- DATA POINTS ---
- Created by: Edward de Bono
- Type: Parallel thinking framework
- Best for: Group decision making and creative sessions
- Key Benefit: Separates ego from thinking mode

ID: FP-NODE-009
TAGS: Decomposition, Idea Generation, First Principles Method
DATE: 2026-03-22
CONFIDENCE: High
--- SYNOPSIS ---
The Decomposition Ladder is a structured first principles method that
strips a problem to its irreducible facts and rebuilds a solution upward
from those facts, challenging every assumption at each step.
--- CORE ANALYSIS ---
# ROOT CAUSE: Why Inherited Solutions Are Expensive
Existing solutions carry the cost of every assumption made by previous
designers. Decomposition removes those inherited costs by starting from
physical and logical bedrock.
# PATTERN: Strip Then Rebuild
Every assumption is made explicit before being challenged.
What remains after all assumptions are removed is the true starting point.
The rebuild asks at every step whether there is a better way given only
what is known to be fundamentally true.
# THE STEPS
Step 1: State the problem in plain language.
Step 2: List every assumption embedded in that statement.
Step 3: Challenge each assumption — is this necessarily true?
Step 4: Identify the irreducible facts that remain.
Step 5: Rebuild the solution upward from those facts.
Step 6: At each step ask if there is a better way given only known truths.
--- DATA POINTS ---
- Famous Example: Tesla battery cost — buying raw materials vs assembled packs
- Famous Example: SpaceX — rethinking rocket cost from materials up
- Key Output: Solutions unconstrained by inherited assumptions

ID: FP-NODE-010
TAGS: Thought Experiments, Reasoning, Einstein, Newton
DATE: 2026-03-22
CONFIDENCE: High
--- SYNOPSIS ---
Thought experiments are imaginary scenarios used to test the logical
consequences of principles without requiring physical experimentation.
They are a core tool of first principles reasoning.
--- CORE ANALYSIS ---
# ROOT CAUSE: When Physical Experiments Are Impossible
Some questions cannot be tested physically — either because of scale,
cost, ethics, or physical impossibility. Thought experiments substitute
logical necessity for empirical observation.
# PATTERN: Isolate, Construct, Follow, Conclude
Isolate one variable or principle. Construct an imaginary scenario that
makes its consequences visible. Follow the logic without flinching.
Ask what the conclusion implies about reality.
# FAMOUS EXAMPLES
Einstein imagined riding alongside a beam of light — leading to special relativity.
Newton imagined a cannon on a mountain firing horizontally until the
cannonball orbited the Earth — leading to the theory of gravity.
Schrodinger imagined a cat in a box to expose the paradox of quantum superposition.
--- DATA POINTS ---
- Used by: Einstein, Newton, Schrodinger, Galileo, Darwin
- Requirement: Logical consistency within the imagined scenario
- Output: Conclusions that constrain what reality must be like

ID: FP-NODE-011
TAGS: Confirmation Bias, Thinking Errors, Cognitive Bias
DATE: 2026-03-22
CONFIDENCE: High
--- SYNOPSIS ---
Confirmation bias is the tendency to seek, interpret, and remember
information in a way that confirms existing beliefs. It is one of the
most pervasive obstacles to clear first principles thinking.
--- CORE ANALYSIS ---
# ROOT CAUSE: The Brain's Efficiency Problem
The brain treats consistency as evidence of truth. Beliefs that have
survived before feel safe. Challenging them requires energy the brain
would rather save. Confirmation bias is the brain optimizing for comfort
over accuracy.
# PATTERN: The Echo Chamber Loop
Seek confirming evidence → feel more certain → seek more confirming
evidence → feel even more certain. The loop tightens until contradictory
evidence feels like an attack rather than information.
# HOW FIRST PRINCIPLES CORRECTS IT
First principles demands that all assumptions be stated explicitly and
then challenged. Making assumptions visible makes them harder to protect
unconsciously. The discipline of justifying every step from bedrock
breaks the confirmation loop.
--- DATA POINTS ---
- Risk Level: Very high in high-stakes decisions
- Correction: State assumptions explicitly before defending them
- Correction: Actively seek disconfirming evidence
- Related Error: Motivated reasoning, sunk cost fallacy

ID: FP-NODE-012
TAGS: Notable Thinkers, Feynman, Munger, Musk, Aristotle
DATE: 2026-03-22
CONFIDENCE: High
--- SYNOPSIS ---
Several notable thinkers have built their methods explicitly on first
principles reasoning, each applying it to a different domain and producing
distinctly different but structurally similar breakthroughs.
--- CORE ANALYSIS ---
# PATTERN: Domain Independence of First Principles
The same method — strip to fundamentals, reason upward — produces
breakthroughs in philosophy, physics, investing, and engineering.
The domain changes but the structure of thinking does not.
# ARISTOTLE
Identified first principles as the foundation of logical demonstration.
His Posterior Analytics defines scientific knowledge as derived from
first principles through valid inference. The origin of the concept.
# RICHARD FEYNMAN
Insisted on understanding from first principles rather than received
knowledge. His Feynman Technique mirrors the approach: explain in plain
language, identify where explanation breaks down, return to source, rebuild.
# CHARLIE MUNGER
Built a latticework of mental models derived from first principles across
multiple disciplines. His investing approach explicitly identifies the
fundamental forces at work rather than following market convention.
# ELON MUSK
Describes first principles as the basis for SpaceX and Tesla.
SpaceX: rethought rocket cost from raw materials up.
Tesla: rethought battery cost from raw materials up.
Both broke industry cost assumptions by refusing to accept inherited pricing.
--- DATA POINTS ---
- Aristotle: Philosophy and logic, 300 BC
- Descartes: Philosophy, cogito ergo sum as bedrock certainty
- Feynman: Physics and education, Feynman Technique
- Munger: Investing, latticework of mental models
- Musk: Engineering, SpaceX and Tesla cost revolutions
- Shannon: Information theory derived entirely from first principles

ID: FP-NODE-013
TAGS: Everyday Application, Finance, Health, Learning, Technology
DATE: 2026-03-22
CONFIDENCE: High
--- SYNOPSIS ---
First principles thinking applies directly to everyday domains including
personal finance, health, learning, cooking, and technology adoption,
producing insights that conventional advice misses.
--- CORE ANALYSIS ---
# PERSONAL FINANCE
The fundamental truth: wealth accumulates when assets generate more value
than liabilities consume. Every financial strategy is just a method to
achieve that equation. Index funds, real estate, and side income are all
implementations of the same first principle.
# LEARNING
The goal is durable transferable mental models, not accumulated facts.
Understanding why something works is more valuable than memorizing that
it works. Spaced repetition and retrieval practice are derived from this.
# HEALTH
Root cause analysis in health asks not what the symptom is but what
system failure is producing the symptom. This shifts from symptom
management to root cause intervention — the core tension in modern medicine.
# TECHNOLOGY ADOPTION
Pattern recognition shows new technologies follow consistent adoption curves.
Innovators, early adopters, early majority, late majority, laggards.
Recognizing where a technology sits predicts when to adopt, invest, or build.
# COOKING
First principles asks what food is doing at a chemical level.
The Maillard reaction explains browning. Understanding it allows application
beyond recipes to any high-heat cooking situation.
--- DATA POINTS ---
- Finance First Principle: Assets minus liabilities equals wealth direction
- Learning First Principle: Models beat facts for long-term retention
- Health First Principle: Treat causes not symptoms
- Technology First Principle: Adoption follows predictable curves
